Why we made this

Threlkeld is surrounded by living things most of us walk past without noticing — birds calling from the hedges, bats hunting over the beck at night, red squirrels in the trees. This page connects children to that wildlife using real data gathered in the village, not a textbook. When a child sees that a robin was heard here, today, science stops being abstract and becomes something happening on their doorstep.

It's built around one of the school's own values — Curiosity — and around the idea of citizen science: children aren't just told about nature, they're looking at the same evidence a real researcher would, and asking the same questions.

What it is

A set of sensors runs continuously in Threlkeld:

  • Bird detector — a microphone listens all day; AI (BirdNET) identifies each species from its song.
  • Bat detector — an ultrasonic microphone listens at night; AI (BatDetect2) identifies bats from calls too high for us to hear.
  • Wildlife camera — spots red squirrels at a feeder and identifies them automatically.
  • Fell camera — a live view of the mountains above the village, with a one-tap "watch yesterday" time-lapse of the whole day.
  • Weather & sky — live temperature, conditions, rainfall and moon phase, which help explain what the animals are doing.
  • River level — the Glenderamackin's height (Years 3–6 only), shown purely as a water-cycle story: rain on the fells runs off into the river hours later.

The page presents this live data three ways, pitched by age — Little Explorers (Reception–Y2), Nature Detectives (Y3–4) and Wild Scientists (Y5–6). Each band has a daily nature diary, today's weather & sky, a "this season" note, a daily challenge, a rotating "did you know?" fact, real photographs, and real bird and bat recordings made in the village — children can tap a species heard today and hear the actual call our sensors picked up. The two older bands also get a river/water-cycle card and a "what a scientist would record" data box — yesterday's complete figures (birds, species, bats, temperatures, rainfall and river level) that children can copy or print as a weekly grid and graph themselves.

Honest status: everything here is live — photos, the diary, weather & sky, seasons, daily challenges, the fell camera, and real bird- and bat-call playback. Bird and bat sounds play for species detected here that we hold a recording for, and the collection grows over time. In the data box, a dash means that day's figure isn't in the record yet ("coming soon"), not an error — each new day is recorded complete.

Curriculum hooks

  • Science — living things & habitats: identifying and grouping birds, bats, mammals; what a habitat provides; why Threlkeld suits red squirrels.
  • Science — animals including humans: food chains (leaf → insect → bat → ...), nocturnal vs diurnal, adaptation (echolocation, camouflage).
  • Science — seasonal change: how bird and bat activity shifts with weather and time of year — the daily diary and "this season" notes make this visible over time.
  • Science — the water cycle: the river card (Years 3–6) shows rain on Blencathra running off the fells into the Glenderamackin hours later — the water cycle happening on the children's doorstep.
  • Maths — data handling (a core strand): the "what a scientist would record" box gives yesterday's complete figures to copy or print as a weekly grid. Children build their own tables, choose appropriate charts (bar charts for counts, line graphs for temperature), calculate ranges and averages, and hunt for relationships between variables.
  • Computing — AI & how computers sense the world: how a sound becomes a spectrogram and how AI recognises a species; a real, local example of machine learning.
  • Geography & local study: our village, the Blencathra landscape, why this place is special for wildlife.
  • Writing: nature diary entries, persuasive writing on conservation, fact-files on a chosen species.

Lesson ideas

  • Daily 5-minute starter: read today's nature diary together, then tackle the day's challenge.
  • Week-long data study (Years 3–6): print the weekly data grid; each morning children record yesterday's figures, then at week's end graph a column of their choice and describe the pattern. A complete data-handling cycle with real local data.
  • Prediction & check: predict tomorrow's busiest bird or whether rain will mean fewer detections — check the next day.
  • Tally & compare: children run their own 5-minute bird survey from the window, then compare with the sensors.
  • Species fact-file: pick a bird, bat or the red squirrel and research it, using the photo and data as a starting point.
  • Cross-year: older children explain the AI/echolocation science to younger ones.

🦇 The hidden night world

Bats call far too high for human ears — but not too high for a computer.

🔬 Ultrasonic detectives

Bats hunt using echolocation — they shout and listen for echoes to find tiny insects in total darkness. Their calls are ultrasonic: too high-pitched for us to hear. Our detector records these secret sounds, and an AI called BatDetect2 works out which species made each call. Last night it counted … bat passes over Threlkeld.

🔬 How does it work?

Microphones listen all day and all night. By day, a program called BirdNET turns each sound into a picture of it (a spectrogram) and uses artificial intelligence to work out which bird made it — a bit like how you recognise a friend's voice. After dark, a second detector with a special ultrasonic microphone listens for bats, and an AI called BatDetect2 identifies each species from calls far too high-pitched for us to hear.
